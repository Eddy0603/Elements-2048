Discovery date Prehistoric
Discovered by
Origin of the Name The name is derived from the Latin ‘carbo’, charcoal
Allotropes Diamond, graphite, graphene, amorphous, fullerene

Group 4  Melting point Sublimes at 3825°C at RTP
Period 2  Boiling point
Atomic number 6  Electron configuration 2, 4
Relative atomic mass 12.011   Density (g cm−3) 3.513 (diamond); 2.2 (graphite) 
Toxicity Non-Toxic Appearance Diamond - Colourless Graphite - black and shiny
Smell Radioactivity Stable (Except 14C)

Uses
  • Hydrocarbons used as fuels
  • Impure carbon in the form of charcoal and coke is used in metal smelting
  • Graphite is used in pencils
  • Carbon fibre is very strong, yet lightweight, material
  • Used in tennis rackets, skis, fishing rods, rockets and aeroplanes
  • Industrial diamonds are used for cutting rocks and drilling
  • Carbon Nanotubes
Abundance
  • Carbon is found in the sun and other stars, formed from the debris of a previous supernova
  • It is built up by nuclear fusion in bigger stars
  • Found in all living things
